# HTK COACH QUICKSTART

Purpose: help a newly hired HTK coach become operational after a 15-minute review.

This quickstart is built from the completed HTK Coaching Operations Repository. It is not a replacement for manager training, but it contains the rules a coach needs to deliver the HTK experience safely and consistently from day one.

---

## 1. HTK Positioning

HTK develops:

- Explosiveness
- Endurance
- Mobility
- Athleticism
- Functional strength
- Durability
- Real-world performance

HTK serves:

- Athletes
- Former athletes
- Military
- Veterans
- First responders
- Performance-focused fitness enthusiasts
- High performers

HTK is not:

- Bodybuilding-only coaching
- A physique-first transformation challenge
- A random workout subscription
- Medical care
- Physical therapy
- Injury rehabilitation
- Pain diagnosis
- A place for clients who want results without accountability

Use this positioning:

> HTK is performance coaching for people who want to be strong, conditioned, mobile, athletic, and capable in real life. It is built around athleticism and durability, not bodybuilding-only training.

If a client cares about appearance, do not shame it. Reframe the conversation around performance, consistency, durability, and training quality.

## 2. Coaching Philosophy

HTK builds capable bodies. A hard session is not automatically a useful session.

Core principles:

- Performance first. Every program decision must connect to the client's goal.
- Strength matters, but strength alone is incomplete.
- Mobility supports better positions, better output, and lower friction in training.
- Conditioning must match the client's sport, work, lifestyle, or readiness demand.
- Explosive work must stay crisp. If speed, intent, landing quality, or control breaks down, reduce complexity, volume, or intensity.
- Progress comes from execution, communication, recovery, and accountability.
- The client is a system: sleep, stress, schedule, nutrition habits, recovery, injury history, and motivation all affect training response.

Before changing a plan, ask:

1. What is the client's primary goal?
2. What does the data say?
3. What did the client report?
4. What is the limiting factor?
5. Is this a programming problem, execution problem, recovery problem, or scope issue?
6. What is the smallest effective adjustment?
7. Does this protect the HTK standard?

## 3. Coach Responsibilities

The coach delivers the HTK coaching experience after enrollment.

You are responsible for:

- Reviewing onboarding information.
- Completing the initial assessment process.
- Setting clear client goals and success markers.
- Designing or assigning programming that matches HTK standards.
- Adjusting training based on performance, readiness, constraints, and check-ins.
- Reviewing weekly check-ins.
- Tracking progress.
- Maintaining accountability.
- Communicating clearly and professionally.
- Escalating injuries, medical questions, brand-risk issues, and client-service concerns.
- Updating the CRM.
- Protecting the HTK coaching standard.

You do not:

- Diagnose injuries.
- Treat pain.
- Provide physical therapy.
- Prescribe medical care.
- Give nutrition plans outside approved scope.
- Guarantee outcomes.
- Ignore safety flags.
- Debate clients emotionally.
- Shame clients.
- Make unapproved promises about refunds, extensions, discounts, or results.

Daily workflow:

1. Review new client assignments.
2. Review overdue onboarding items.
3. Review client messages.
4. Review check-ins due today.
5. Handle urgent escalations first.
6. Review training data and client notes.
7. Send required coaching responses.
8. Update programming only when a clear reason exists.
9. Update CRM.
10. Flag risks for manager.

Response standards:

| Situation | Required action | Deadline |
|---|---|---:|
| New client assigned | Review handoff and onboarding | Same business day |
| Check-in submitted | Review and respond | Within 1 business day |
| Client message | Respond or acknowledge | Within 1 business day |
| Injury or pain question | Stop coaching advice and escalate | Same day |
| Missed check-in | Follow missed check-in protocol | Same day due |

4. Program Build

## 6. Weekly Check-In Process

Weekly check-ins monitor execution, progress, recovery, obstacles, and accountability.

Workflow:

1. Client submits check-in.
2. Coach reviews adherence.
3. Coach reviews training notes.
4. Coach reviews recovery and readiness.
5. Coach reviews goal progress.
6. Coach identifies blockers.
7. Coach decides: continue, adjust, regress, escalate, or accountability intervention.
8. Coach responds with clear feedback.
9. Coach updates CRM.

Required check-in questions:

- What sessions did you complete?
- What sessions did you miss?
- What felt strong?
- What felt limited?
- What was the biggest obstacle this week?
- How was recovery?
- Any pain, injury, or unusual symptoms?
- What is your focus for next week?

Decision rules:

| Decision | Use when |
|---|---|
| Continue | Most work completed, recovery acceptable, no safety flag, progress stable |
| Adjust | Work completed but recovery poor, schedule changed, progress stalls with good adherence |
| Regress | Repeated missed work, sessions too difficult, readiness/technique not supporting plan |
| Escalate | Injury/pain concern, medical advice request, repeated non-compliance, complaint, unsafe behavior |

Response format:

1. Acknowledge execution.
2. Name the most important pattern.
3. Give one or two priorities.
4. State any program adjustment.
5. Reinforce next week's standard.

A check-in is complete only when:

- Coach reviewed it.
- Client received feedback.
- Any program change is documented.
- Any missed-session pattern is noted.
- Any injury/safety flag is escalated.
- CRM is updated.

## 7. Program Design Standards

Every program must be based on:

- Primary goal
- Training age
- Current capacity
- Schedule
- Equipment
- Mobility needs
- Conditioning needs
- Strength needs
- Recovery constraints
- Injury/scope flags
- Client adherence history

Every active HTK program must include, unless a documented reason says otherwise:

1. Strength development
2. Conditioning or endurance work
3. Mobility or movement preparation
4. Athletic or explosive exposure when appropriate
5. Recovery/readiness awareness
6. Progression method
7. Weekly execution target

Program build workflow:

1. Confirm goal category.
2. Confirm available training days.
3. Select weekly structure.
4. Choose primary adaptations.
5. Select exercise categories.
6. Set volume and intensity conservatively enough for adherence.
7. Add mobility and preparation work.
8. Add conditioning matched to goal.
9. Add explosive work only when readiness supports it.
10. Define progression rules.
11. Define check-in metrics.
12. Document rationale in CRM.

Progress when:

- Client completes prescribed work.
- Technique is acceptable.
- Recovery is adequate.
- Check-in confirms readiness.
- No safety flag is present.

Hold steady when:

- Client barely completes work.
- RPE is consistently high.
- Sleep/recovery is poor.
- Adherence is inconsistent.
- Movement quality is unstable.

Regress when:

- Client cannot complete sessions.
- Pain/safety concern appears.
- Technique breaks down.
- Client misses repeated sessions.
- Load or complexity exceeds readiness.

Do not change a program because:

- Client is bored.
- One session felt hard.
- Coach wants novelty.
- Social media trends suggest it.
- Client asks for random exercises.

Change a program when:

- Goal changes.
- Assessment data changes.
- Adherence pattern requires adjustment.
- Recovery is not supporting the plan.
- Client progresses beyond current stimulus.
- Safety or scope issue requires modification.

## 8. Accountability Framework

Accountability means clear standards, direct feedback, and documented follow-through. It does not mean shame or hype.

Accountability levels:

| Level | Use when | Coach action |
|---|---|---|
| Level 1: Standard | Client is compliant | Reinforce execution and clarify next priority |
| Level 2: Early Drift | Client misses 1-2 sessions or gives vague check-ins | Identify obstacle, reduce complexity if needed, set one non-negotiable action |
| Level 3: Pattern | Client repeatedly misses sessions, skips check-ins, or does not communicate | Name pattern, ask for decision, adjust to minimum effective version if appropriate |
| Level 4: Review | Client continues not executing after direct accountability | Follow non-compliant protocol, escalate if needed, note retention risk |

Accountability conversation:

1. State the observed pattern.
2. Connect it to the goal.
3. Ask what is blocking execution.
4. Set one clear action.
5. Confirm client agreement.
6. Document in CRM.

Use this language:

> You missed two of the three planned sessions this week. The issue is not one bad week; the issue is that your goal depends on consistency. What blocked execution, and what needs to change next week?

Minimum effective week:

- Use when life is busy but the client still wants progress.
- Define minimum sessions, shortened session option, non-negotiable mobility or conditioning dose, and check-in requirement.
- Rule: minimum effective work is better than disappearing.

Escalate when:

- Client is non-compliant for multiple weeks.
- Client becomes hostile.
- Client blames coach while not executing.
- Client wants refund, pause, or exception.

## 9. Injury Escalation Rules

Core rule: HTK coaches do not diagnose, treat, rehab, or give medical advice.

Escalate immediately if the client reports:

- Acute injury
- Sharp pain
- Pain that changes movement
- Numbness, tingling, radiating symptoms, or loss of function
- Chest pain, faintness, severe shortness of breath, or concerning systemic symptoms
- Head injury or suspected concussion
- Medical diagnosis requiring training restrictions
- Healthcare provider instructions the coach does not understand
- Request for diagnosis, treatment, rehab, or medical interpretation

Use this response:

> I cannot give medical advice or diagnose that. Stop the movement that triggered it for now, and get guidance from a qualified healthcare professional if symptoms are concerning or continue. I am escalating this so we handle training appropriately.

Injury escalation workflow:

1. Stop giving programming advice for the injured area or concerning symptom.
2. Ask only operational safety questions:
   - Did this happen during training?
   - Have you been cleared to continue training?
   - Are there movements a healthcare professional told you to avoid?
3. Refer client to qualified medical guidance when appropriate.
4. Notify manager.
5. Record objective facts in CRM.
6. Modify unaffected training only if safe and within scope.
7. Resume affected training only after clearance or manager-approved pathway.

Coach may:

- Pause an exercise.
- Substitute a non-painful movement pattern if no medical red flag exists.
- Reduce load, volume, range, complexity, or intensity.
- Program around unaffected areas when appropriate.
- Ask whether client has medical clearance.
- Document what client reports.

Coach may not:

- Diagnose.
- Treat.
- Prescribe rehab.
- Tell client to ignore pain.
- Interpret imaging or medical tests.
- Override healthcare provider guidance.
- Promise HTK can fix the issue.

## 10. Scope Of Practice Rules

Coaches may:

- Design performance-focused training.
- Adjust exercises, volume, intensity, and progression.
- Coach exercise execution.
- Provide accountability.
- Discuss training habits.
- Discuss recovery behaviors in general terms.
- Track progress.
- Refer clients to qualified professionals when needed.
- Communicate with manager about client risk.

Coaches may not:

- Diagnose medical conditions.
- Treat injuries.
- Prescribe rehab.
- Interpret pain medically.
- Interpret lab results, imaging, or medical tests.
- Prescribe medication or supplements as treatment.
- Give therapy or mental health counseling.
- Give legal, financial, or medical advice.
- Override medical restrictions.

Nutrition boundary:

- General habits are allowed: protein consistency, hydration, meal consistency, fueling around training, sleep, and recovery routines.
- Do not prescribe medical nutrition therapy, treat disease with diet, create restrictive diets for medical conditions, diagnose eating disorders, or manage supplements as medical treatment.

Use this referral language:

> That is outside my scope as a coach. You need to speak with a qualified healthcare professional for medical guidance. I can adjust training once we know what is appropriate.

If unsure:

- Do not guess.
- Escalate to manager.
- Document the client question, coach response, referral/escalation, training change, and next action.

CRM must let any authorized HTK team member understand client status, risk, progress, and next action.

Required fields:

| Field | Requirement |
|---|---|
| Client info | ID, name, email, approved phone, coach |
| Client profile | Segment, offer, start date |
| Goals | Primary goal, secondary goal, main obstacle |
| Training context | Background, schedule constraints, equipment |
| Safety | Injury flag, clearance status |
| Coaching status | Current stage, check-in status, weekly adherence, accountability level |
| Progress | Progress marker, program block |
| Operations | Next action, next action date, retention risk, notes |

Approved stages:

1. Enrolled
2. Onboarding
3. Assessment Pending
4. Active Coaching
5. Progress Review
6. Renewal Window
7. Paused
8. Completed
9. Canceled
10. Escalated

Update CRM when:

- Client completes onboarding.
- Assessment is completed.
- Goal is set or changed.
- Program block changes.
- Check-in is reviewed.
- Client misses check-in.
- Client becomes non-compliant.
- Injury/safety flag appears.
- Progress review is completed.
- Renewal conversation occurs.
- Client pauses, cancels, completes, or renews.

Notes must be factual, brief, actionable, and connected to goal, adherence, progress, or risk.

Daily CRM audit:

- Every message requiring action has next action.
- Every reviewed check-in is logged.
- Every missed check-in is logged.
- Every injury flag is escalated.
- Every program change has a rationale.

- Hype-driven
- Desperate

- Medical

- HTK prevents injury.

> Would HTK be comfortable if this exact message were shown publicly with the brand name attached?
