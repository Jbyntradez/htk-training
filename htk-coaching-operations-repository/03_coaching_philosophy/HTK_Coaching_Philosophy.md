# 3. HTK Coaching Philosophy

## Core Philosophy

HTK builds capable bodies. The client should become stronger, better conditioned, more mobile, more athletic, and more durable for real life.

Training should develop performance, not just fatigue. A hard session is not automatically a useful session.

## Coaching Principles

### 1. Performance First

Every program decision must connect to a performance goal:

- Move better.
- Produce force.
- Sustain effort.
- Recover well.
- Handle real-world demands.
- Build repeatable capacity.

### 2. Strength Is Necessary But Not Sufficient

HTK values strength, but strength alone is incomplete. The coach must also consider conditioning, mobility, coordination, movement quality, and durability.

### 3. Mobility Supports Performance

Mobility is not filler. Mobility work should support better positions, better output, and lower friction in training.

### 4. Conditioning Must Match The Goal

Endurance work must support the client's sport, work, lifestyle, or readiness demand. Conditioning is not random punishment.

### 5. Power Requires Quality

Explosive work must be crisp. If speed, intent, landing quality, or control breaks down, the coach reduces complexity, volume, or intensity.

### 6. Progress Comes From Execution

The best program fails if the client does not execute. Coaching includes accountability, communication, and habit correction.

### 7. The Client Is A System

Training response is affected by:

- Sleep
- Stress
- Schedule
- Work demands
- Nutrition habits
- Recovery
- Injury history
- Motivation
- Environment

The coach adjusts the plan based on the whole picture, not just the spreadsheet.

## Programming Priorities

HTK programming should balance:

1. Goal specificity.
2. Movement quality.
3. Strength development.
4. Conditioning development.
5. Mobility and durability.
6. Recovery and readiness.
7. Client adherence.

## What Good Coaching Looks Like

Good HTK coaching is:

- Clear
- Direct
- Specific
- Measurable
- Accountable
- Adaptable
- Professional
- Performance-driven

Bad HTK coaching is:

- Random workouts
- Excessive novelty
- Bodybuilding-only bias
- Ignoring check-ins
- Training through pain without escalation
- Overcomplicating simple problems
- Letting clients drift without accountability
- Making promises the brand cannot support

## Coach Filter

Before changing a plan, ask:

1. What is the client's primary goal?
2. What does the data say?
3. What did the client report?
4. What is the limiting factor?
5. Is this a programming problem, execution problem, recovery problem, or scope issue?
6. What is the smallest effective adjustment?
7. Does this protect the HTK standard?
